Nagara Style (Northern India):

The Nagara style is the dominant temple architecture in Northern India, characterized by its towering spire (shikhara) and a focus on intricate carvings and the curved profile of the structure. Nagara temples are often built on a raised stone platform with steps leading up to the entrance, and they typically lack elaborate boundary walls or gateways.

Features of Nagara Style:

Shikhara:

The most distinctive feature is the shikhara, a towering, pyramidal spire that rises above the garbha griha (sanctum sanctorum). The shikhara is often described as a representation of Mount Meru, the mythical center of the universe.

Garbha Griha:

The central chamber where the deity is housed is located directly beneath the tallest shikhara.

Mandapa:

Nagara temples often include one or more mandapas (halls) for worship and circumambulation.

Amalaka:

A large, circular stone slab that crowns the shikhara, also known as the kalasha.

Circumambulatory Passage:

A passage around the garbha griha allows devotees to walk around the deity.

Intricate Carvings:

Nagara temples are often adorned with elaborate carvings and sculptures on their walls and pillars.
