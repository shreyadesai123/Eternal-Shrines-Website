Vesara Style (Deccan Region):

The Vesara style of temple architecture is a hybrid style that emerged in the Deccan region, primarily in Karnataka, blending elements of the Nagara style of North India and the Dravidian style of South India. It's also known as the "Hybridized style". Vesara temples often feature a bell-shaped superstructure and intricate carvings.

Features of the Vesara Style:

1. Fusion of Nagara and Dravida Styles:

Vesara architecture combines the vertical, pyramidal shikhara (tower) of the Nagara style with the horizontal, spacious mandapa (hall) of the Dravida style.
The superstructure (tower) is typically bell-shaped and shorter than the Nagara shikhara, and the walls are often circular or straight-sided cones.
A connecting vestibule (antarala) links the sanctum and mandapa, reflecting the hybrid design.

2. Unique Elements:

Stellate Plan:

The ground plan is often star-shaped (stellate).

Intricate Carvings:

Pillars, doorways, and ceilings are adorned with elaborate carvings depicting scenes from mythology and daily life.

Jagati (Platform):

Structures are built on an unraised platform or Jagati as their base.

Open Ambulatory Passageway:

Some Vesara temples feature an open ambulatory passageway, a unique feature in Indian architecture.

3. Key Elements and Their Transformations:

Shikhara:

The Nagara-style tower or superstructure above the sanctum.

Mandapa:

The main temple hall, often featuring a spacious design with intricately carved pillars.

Antarala:

A connecting vestibule between the sanctum and mandapa.

Pillars:

Each pillar is often uniquely carved, representing themes of mythology and daily life.

Gavaksas:

Ornamental arches on the shikhara, often with a horseshoe shape.

Amalakas:

The ribbed disc-shaped part that sits atop the tower.

Stupi:

A miniature representation of the stupa at the summit of the tower.
