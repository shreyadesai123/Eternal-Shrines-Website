Dravida Style (Southern India):

Dravidian architecture, a prominent style of Hindu temple architecture in South India, is characterized by towering gopurams (gateways), intricate pillared halls, and stepped vimanas (towers). It flourished from the 8th to 13th centuries CE. Key features include enclosed compound walls, entrance gateways (gopurams), and subsidiary shrines within the main tower or alongside it.

Features of Dravidian Architecture:

Gopurams:

These are the most prominent feature of Dravidian temples, serving as grand gateways to the temple complex.

Vimanas:

The main temple tower is a stepped pyramid, unlike the curving shikhara of the Nagara style.

Mandapas:

Intricately carved pillared halls, often surrounding the inner sanctum (garbhagriha).

Compound Walls:

The entire temple complex is enclosed by a wall, creating a distinct architectural unit.

Temple Tanks:

Often found within the compound, serving a religious purpose.

Subsidiary Shrines:

Smaller shrines may be found within or near the main tower.

Garbhagriha:

The inner sanctum is usually located in the smallest tower, often the oldest part of the temple.
